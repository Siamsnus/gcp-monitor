Background

What Is the Global Consciousness Project?

The Global Consciousness Project (GCP) is one of the longest-running and most unusual scientific experiments in the world. Since August 1998, a network of hardware random number generators — whimsically called "eggs" — has been running continuously at host sites across every inhabited continent. Each egg produces 200 random bits every second using quantum tunneling, a process that is, by the laws of physics, completely unpredictable.

The project was created by Roger Nelson at Princeton University's PEAR Lab (Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research), which had spent the previous two decades studying whether human consciousness could influence random systems in laboratory settings. The GCP extended this question to a global scale: if individual minds can slightly bias a random number generator under controlled conditions, what happens when millions of minds focus on the same event?

The answer, according to the GCP's data, is that the network's random output becomes slightly less random during events that engage mass human attention. Over 500 formally tested events spanning more than 25 years, the cumulative statistical result shows a departure from chance with odds reported as greater than a trillion to one.

What this means — whether it's evidence of some interconnected field of consciousness, an artifact of how events are selected and analyzed, or something else entirely — remains deeply debated. The project doesn't claim to have proven global consciousness exists. It claims to have found an anomaly in the data that deserves continued investigation.

The Hardware

Each "egg" is a physical random number generator based on quantum tunneling — not software pseudorandomness. These are dedicated hardware devices connected to host computers that record one 200-bit sample every second, 24/7. The data is transmitted to a central server and archived. The entire dataset since 1998 is publicly available for independent analysis.

The Statistics

The primary measure is the Stouffer Z-score, which combines data from all active eggs into a single statistic. When this exceeds expected values, it means the network — as a whole — is producing output that deviates from pure randomness. The "network variance" displayed above reflects this: a value of 1.0 is expected, and anything above suggests more structure than chance predicts.

The Controversy

Skeptics raise legitimate concerns. The selection of "global events" involves human judgment, which introduces potential bias. The effect sizes are tiny. Some independent analyses of the same data reach different conclusions. The GCP itself acknowledges these challenges. This site presents the data as-is — draw your own conclusions.

September 11 Attacks

The most studied and debated event in GCP history. The network showed statistically significant deviations that appeared to begin several hours before the first plane struck the World Trade Center. The cumulative deviation continued through the day and into the following days.

This apparent "precursor" effect is the most controversial claim — critics argue it results from selective time window analysis, while proponents note the deviation was one of the strongest ever recorded. An independent analysis published in Foundations of Physics Letters confirmed the statistical significance but noted the interpretation remains open.

The magnitude 9.1 earthquake that triggered the devastating Indian Ocean tsunami coincided with anomalous network behavior. This event killed over 230,000 people across 14 countries, making it one of the deadliest natural disasters in recorded history.

The GCP data showed elevated coherence around the time of the earthquake itself — before the scale of the disaster was understood by most of the world. This raises interesting questions about whether the effect relates to human awareness of events or to the events themselves.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is This Real?

What exactly am I looking at on this page?
The gauge shows the current "network variance" — a statistical measure of how much 65+ hardware random number generators around the world are deviating from expected random behavior. A value of 1.0 is normal. Values significantly above 1.0 suggest the network is producing output with more structure than pure chance would predict. The timeline shows how this has changed over the past 24 hours, and the map shows the location and individual status of each generator.
Does this actually prove global consciousness exists?
No — and the project's own researchers are careful not to claim that. What the data shows is a statistical anomaly: the network's output correlates with global events more than chance predicts, with cumulative odds reported as greater than a trillion to one. But statistical significance doesn't equal proof of a mechanism. The anomaly could be evidence of interconnected consciousness, or it could reflect methodological issues like event selection bias, or something else entirely. The honest answer is that nobody knows yet.
What do skeptics say about the GCP?
The main critiques are: (1) Event selection involves subjective human judgment — you choose which events to test after seeing the data, which can introduce bias. (2) The statistical methods have been questioned by independent researchers, some of whom find no significant effect when using different analytical approaches. (3) There's no physical mechanism that could explain how human consciousness would affect electronic devices. (4) The effect sizes are extremely small. The GCP's own FAQ addresses many of these concerns, and the project makes all data publicly available for independent verification.
Can I predict events by watching the gauge?
No. The deviations are subtle statistical trends that only become meaningful when analyzed formally over defined time periods. Random fluctuations happen constantly, and any individual spike is not meaningful on its own. The GCP itself cautions against reading too much into real-time displays. Think of it more like a long-term weather pattern than a moment-by-moment forecast.
What about the claim that generators reacted before 9/11?
This is the most famous — and most disputed — GCP claim. The network data does show deviations beginning several hours before the first attack. Proponents argue this suggests a form of collective precognition or a non-local consciousness effect. Critics point out that the analysis window was defined after the fact, and that in any dataset spanning years of continuous recording, you can find apparent "precursor" patterns by chance if you look hard enough. The statistical significance was confirmed independently, but the interpretation remains deeply contested.
Where does the data come from?
The raw data comes directly from the Global Consciousness Project's public data feeds at global-mind.org (formerly hosted at Princeton University). This site fetches the GCP Dot coherence data every 10 minutes and displays it in a more accessible format. The GCP makes its entire 25+ year database freely available for download and independent analysis — all the raw numbers, analysis code, and event registry are public.
What's the difference between GCP 1.0 and GCP 2.0?
The original GCP (1.0) is the Princeton-based project running since 1998 with ~65 dedicated hardware random number generators, directed by Roger Nelson. GCP 2.0 is a newer, separate project run by HeartMath Institute with over 300 "citizen scientist" hosted devices worldwide. GCP 2.0 uses different hardware and methodology but tests a similar hypothesis. This site primarily displays data from the original GCP network.

Historical Research

Princeton PEAR Lab (1979–2007)

The Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research laboratory, where the GCP originated, spent 28 years studying human-machine interaction. Their experiments tested whether human intention could influence the output of random number generators. They published dozens of peer-reviewed papers reporting small but statistically significant effects, though the results were never replicated to mainstream science's satisfaction.

Declassified

CIA Stargate Project (1978–1995)

A US government program that spent $20 million over 20 years investigating "remote viewing" — the alleged ability to perceive distant locations through mental means. While the final evaluation concluded the intelligence applications were not reliable, some of the experimental results were harder to dismiss, leading to ongoing debate about the nature of consciousness and perception.

Established Science

Biophoton Emission

All living cells emit ultra-weak photons (biophotons) — this is established science. The fringe extension of this research proposes that biophotons play a role in cellular communication and even consciousness. Some researchers have suggested connections between biophoton coherence and the kind of "field consciousness" effects the GCP claims to measure.

For Researchers

Technical Details & Data Access

The GCP's entire dataset — over 25 years of continuous, second-by-second random number data from up to 70 devices worldwide — is freely available for download and independent analysis. The project uses a GPL license for all software, and maintains a public registry of every formal event hypothesis tested.

Raw data: Available via CSV extract at global-mind.org/data/extract.html. Each file contains per-second, per-egg trial sums (200-bit samples). Bulk downloads of compressed daily files are available via wget at approximately 20-25 MB per day uncompressed.

Analysis toolkit: John Walker's EGGSHELL suite provides C++ tools for processing the dataset, available at fourmilab.ch/eggtools/eggshell. The toolkit includes data extraction, statistical analysis, and visualization capabilities.

Event registry: Every formal hypothesis test is specified in advance and published in the GCP prediction registry — including event timing, analysis method, and expected effect. This pre-registration is a methodological strength that addresses some concerns about post-hoc analysis.

This site's data: This visualization fetches the GCP Dot coherence data every 10 minutes via an automated process. The per-egg data shown on the map is estimated from the network-level reading. The source code is open at github.com/Siamsnus/gcp-monitor.
