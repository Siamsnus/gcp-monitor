The Experiment in Brief

The Global Consciousness Project (GCP) is an international scientific collaboration running continuously since August 1998. Its setup is simple: a network of hardware random number generators — whimsically called "eggs" — operates at host sites across every inhabited continent. Each egg produces 200 random bits every second using quantum tunneling, a process that is, by the laws of physics, completely unpredictable.

The project's hypothesis is equally straightforward: during events that engage the attention or emotions of large numbers of people simultaneously, the network's output will become slightly less random than expected. Over more than 500 formally tested events spanning 25+ years, the project reports that this is exactly what happens — with cumulative statistical odds against chance of greater than a trillion to one.

What this means — whether it's evidence of some interconnected field of consciousness, an artifact of how events are selected and analyzed, or something else entirely — remains deeply debated. The project doesn't claim to have proven global consciousness exists. It claims to have found an anomaly in the data that deserves continued investigation.

Origins: Princeton's PEAR Lab

The GCP didn't emerge from nowhere. It was born from the Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research laboratory (PEAR), which operated at Princeton University from 1979 to 2007 — one of the longest-running anomalous research programs at any major university.

PEAR was founded by Robert Jahn, then dean of Princeton's School of Engineering and Applied Science. The lab spent 28 years studying whether human consciousness could influence the output of random number generators and other physical systems under controlled laboratory conditions. Over thousands of experiments involving hundreds of participants, PEAR reported finding small but statistically significant effects.

Roger Nelson, who would go on to direct the GCP, was PEAR's coordinator of research. In the 1990s, Nelson began taking random number generators out of the lab and into real-world settings — concerts, theater performances, religious rituals, group meditations. He called these "FieldREG" experiments, and the results suggested that group coherence might amplify the effects seen in individual laboratory sessions.

Two field experiments in 1997 proved pivotal. During a web-organized "Gaiamind Meditation" in January, data from 12 independent random number generators across the US and Europe showed statistically significant deviations. Then, in September, the same network showed anomalous patterns during the worldwide response to Princess Diana's death. These results convinced Nelson and his colleagues that a permanent, global network was worth building.

The EGG network — the Global Consciousness Project — went live in August 1998.

How It Works

The Hardware

Each "egg" is a dedicated hardware random number generator based on quantum tunneling — not software pseudorandomness. These are physical devices that exploit the inherent unpredictability of quantum mechanics to produce genuinely random output. Each egg is connected to a host computer that records one 200-bit sample (called a "trial") every second, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

The data is transmitted to a central server and archived. The entire dataset — billions of random trials spanning more than two decades — is publicly available for download and independent analysis. The project uses the GPL license for all software and maintains complete transparency about its methods.

The Statistics

The primary analytical tool is the Stouffer Z-score, which combines data from all active eggs into a single statistic. When the network is operating normally — producing genuinely random output — the expected variance is 1.0. Values significantly above 1.0 suggest the network is producing output with more structure than pure chance predicts.

For each tested event, the GCP defines a time window and a specific prediction in advance. The prediction is registered before the data is examined, and all parameters are fixed beforehand. This pre-registration protocol is a key methodological strength — it prevents the cherry-picking of favorable time windows after seeing the data.

The XOR Problem

A crucial technical detail: the raw bit-streams from the random number generators are processed through an XOR operation before analysis. This is intended to correct for any hardware bias in the generators, but it has an important implication — it means that any simple physical influence on the generators (like electromagnetic interference) would be filtered out. The anomalies the GCP detects, whatever their cause, cannot be easily explained by conventional physical effects on the hardware.

The Protocol

Events and analysis parameters are pre-registered before data examination. Time windows and statistical methods are fixed in advance to prevent post-hoc selection.

The Results

The GCP's cumulative result across all formal tests is striking. The composite statistic — essentially a running total of how much the data deviates from randomness during selected events — shows a steady upward trend. After 15+ years and 500+ events, the departure from expectation reaches approximately 7 sigma, corresponding to odds of more than a trillion to one against chance.

If there were no effect, this line would wander randomly around zero. Instead, it climbs steadily upward, suggesting that something systematic is happening during the selected events.

The effect is not consistent across all event types. Some categories show stronger deviations than others. Natural disasters and terrorist attacks tend to produce the strongest effects. New Year celebrations show a distinctive rolling pattern as midnight sweeps across time zones. Elections and planned events produce more variable results. Some formally tested events show no deviation at all.

This inconsistency is interpreted differently by different camps. Supporters see it as evidence that the effect depends on the intensity and coherence of global emotional engagement. Critics see it as evidence that the positive results are statistical noise amplified by selective attention.

The Controversy

The GCP is not accepted by mainstream science. The claims are extraordinary, and extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. Here are the main criticisms:

Event Selection Bias

The choice of which events to test involves human judgment. Critics argue that even with pre-registration, the broader selection of what constitutes a "global event" is subjective, and this subjectivity could introduce systematic bias.

No Physical Mechanism

There is no known physical mechanism by which human consciousness could influence electronic devices at a distance. The XOR processing makes conventional explanations (electromagnetic interference, power grid fluctuations) even less plausible, but it also makes the claimed effect harder to explain by any known physics.

Independent Analyses Disagree

Peter Bancel, who published a detailed analysis of the GCP data in 2017, concluded that the data did not support the global consciousness hypothesis specifically. Instead, he found the results more consistent with what he called a "goal-oriented effect" — essentially, an experimenter effect rather than a consciousness field effect. Other independent analysts have reached different conclusions.

Effect Size

The effects are extremely small. While the cumulative statistical significance is large (due to the massive dataset), the actual deviations from randomness are tiny — well below what any individual could detect by looking at the raw data. This makes replication and falsification difficult.

Roger Nelson's Own Acknowledgment

Even the project's director has been cautious. In a 2007 interview, Nelson acknowledged that the data was "not solid enough for global consciousness to be said to exist at all." The GCP positions itself as detecting an anomaly worthy of investigation, not as having proven a hypothesis.

GCP 2.0: The Next Generation

In 2023, the HeartMath Institute launched the Global Consciousness Project 2.0, a separate but related initiative. GCP 2.0 uses different hardware — over 300 "citizen scientist" hosted devices worldwide — and a different analytical approach, but tests a similar core hypothesis.

GCP 2.0 places a greater emphasis on intentional participation. While the original GCP is a purely observational experiment (the eggs run passively and don't require anyone to "do" anything), GCP 2.0 actively invites participants to engage in group meditations and heart-coherence exercises, then measures whether these coordinated intentions correlate with changes in the network's random output.

The two projects are independent but complementary. This site primarily displays data from the original GCP network.
